Designing for Clarity in a Complex System

A case study about designing the conditions for alignment, visibility, and product coherence—without disrupting delivery.

Author: Daniela Penati Role: UX Manager Scope: Team + Operating Model + Product Coherence

Key takeaways

  • Designed a lightweight operating rhythm to improve shared visibility and momentum.
  • Expanded authorship while holding a coherent experience direction.
  • Enabled the product to be addressed as an end-to-end system.
1. Context

A Leadership Moment in a Complex System

In early 2024, I stepped into my first people-management role at JPMorgan Chase, transitioning from an individual contributor position into a space that had experienced frequent leadership turnover.

Taking responsibility for the five-person UX team supporting the Build & Release area of the Engineering Portal came with challenges often seen in large organizations undergoing change:

  • A distributed team of early-career designers and researchers with growing domain knowledge, but limited experience operating as a coordinated team within a large, multi-stakeholder environment
  • A complex product space with a fragmented experience shaped by multiple owners
  • Evolving expectations around UX’s role within a PM-centric operating model
  • Ongoing misalignment across Product and Engineering teams that impacted planning and delivery

These conditions were not unique, but they required intentional design leadership. My focus became identifying high-leverage interventions that could improve alignment, visibility, and product coherence—without disrupting delivery.

2. Strategic approach

Designing the Conditions for Impact

Rather than addressing individual symptoms in isolation, I focused on designing the conditions that would allow both the team and the product to progress together.

My approach centered on three deliberate choices:

  • Establish a shared operating rhythm. Create clarity around priorities, sequencing, and ownership—without adding unnecessary process.
  • Make work visible and collaborative. Shift UX engagement from reactive support to active partnership across Product and Engineering.
  • Hold a coherent product vision while expanding authorship. Make space for contributions while ensuring the experience evolved as a system.

Importantly, these choices were as much about what I avoided—heavy governance, disruptive structural change—as what I introduced: small, high-leverage interventions that could scale through practice.

“Structure was never the goal; it was the scaffolding that made flexibility possible.”

3. Execution

Turning Strategy into Practice

With a clear strategic direction in place, the focus shifted to translating intent into consistent, observable practice. I established a predictable weekly cadence that balanced focus, learning, and visibility—allowing the team to build new habits incrementally while continuing to deliver.

One-week cadence (overview)

  • Commitment: intentionally scoped work aligned to capacity
  • Learning: domain knowledge and rationale embedded into delivery
  • Checkpoint: early visibility into progress, risks, and dependencies
  • Demo: work shared, connected to the broader experience, and later extended to stakeholders
4. Product impact

From Fragmentation to Coherence

As the team developed a shared rhythm and clearer operating model, space opened to address the Build & Release experience more holistically. The product could be approached not as isolated surfaces, but as an end-to-end system—with clearer relationships between tools, stages, and user actions.

The resulting designs emphasized clearer sequencing, more consistent interaction patterns, and reduced cognitive load. The experience became calmer—not because it was simplified, but because it was better aligned.

5. Reflection

Designing at Multiple Levels

This experience reinforced a belief I have carried throughout my career: strong design is not defined by control, but by clarity, care, and intention. Stepping into people leadership did not pull me away from product work—it expanded the surface on which design decisions mattered.

Enabling an early-career team to do their best work required designing the conditions in which good decisions could emerge consistently, without constant intervention: introducing structure early and loosening it deliberately, holding a clear product vision while making space for contributions, and knowing when to step in as a designer and when to step back as a leader.

I remain deeply motivated by complex product challenges. What this experience added is a stronger appreciation for how team dynamics, operating models, and design craft are inseparable at scale.

“I still design. I simply do it across more dimensions now.”
